Be Informed

Avoid Costly Apostille Mistakes

Common errors that delay processing, result in rejection, or require starting over.

Submitting Copies When Originals Are Required

Most apostille requests require the original certified document or a certified copy from the issuing government office. Personal photocopies are not accepted.

Using the Wrong Notarization

Not all notarizations are the same. The type of notarization required depends on the document and destination country. Incorrect notarizations cause rejection.

Sending Documents Before Confirming Requirements

Every destination country has different requirements. Sending documents without first confirming what is needed leads to delays and additional costs.

Missing Certified Copies

Vital records must come from the issuing government office. Hospital birth records, unofficial transcripts, and uncertified copies cannot be apostilled.

Incorrect Destination Country Information

The process for apostille authentication varies by issuing state and destination country. Incorrect country information leads to documents processed under the wrong requirements.

Submitting Documents That Are Not Apostille Eligible

Not every document can receive an apostille. Private documents, certain federal records, and foreign-issued documents require a different authentication path.

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common apostille and notarization questions.

An apostille is an official government certification that authenticates a document for use in foreign countries that are members of the Hague Apostille Convention. It verifies the authenticity of the signature, seal, or stamp — not the document's content — so it will be legally recognized abroad.

If your destination country is a Hague Convention member, you need an apostille. If the country is not a member, you need embassy or consulate legalization — a multi-step process. We confirm which process applies to your situation before you submit.
