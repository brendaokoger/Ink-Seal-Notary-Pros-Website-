Apostille Basics

An apostille is an official certification issued by a government authority that authenticates the origin of a public document for use in foreign countries. It's the international equivalent of a notarization and is recognized by all countries that have signed the Hague Convention of 1961. The apostille verifies the authenticity of the signature, seal, or stamp on the document — not the content of the document itself.
Over 120 countries are members of the Hague Apostille Convention and accept apostilled documents. This includes popular destinations such as Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Mexico, Brazil, India, the Philippines, Ukraine, and many more. If you're unsure whether your destination country accepts apostilles, contact us — we'll confirm the requirements for your specific situation.
An apostille is a single-step certification accepted by all Hague Convention member countries. Document authentication (also called "legalization") is a multi-step process required for countries that have not joined the Hague Convention. Authentication typically involves state certification, U.S. Department of State authentication, and then embassy or consulate legalization in the destination country. We handle both processes.
Common documents we apostille include: birth certificates, marriage certificates, divorce decrees, death certificates, adoption records, diplomas and transcripts, background checks, power of attorney, corporate documents, notarized affidavits, and court documents. If you have a document not on this list, reach out and we can advise whether an apostille is appropriate.

Remote Online Notary (RON)

Remote online notarization (RON) allows you to have documents notarized via a secure video call — no in-person meeting required. You connect with a commissioned notary through a secure platform, verify your identity digitally, and sign your documents electronically while on video. The notary witnesses and seals the documents electronically. RON is legally valid in most U.S. states and accepted for many international purposes.
In many cases, yes. Documents notarized via RON can be apostilled, but requirements vary by state and destination country. Some countries accept electronically notarized documents; others require a traditional wet-ink signature. You'll need: a computer, tablet, or smartphone with a camera and microphone; a stable internet connection; a valid government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport); and the document(s) you need notarized in digital form. We'll send you a secure link to join the session and walk you through every step.
RON is currently authorized in the majority of U.S. states, with more states adopting legislation regularly. Even if your home state hasn't yet enacted RON laws, you may still be able to use our RON services depending on the document type and accepting party's requirements. Contact us to confirm availability for your state and document type.
