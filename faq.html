Apostille Basics

An apostille is an official government certification that authenticates a document for use in countries that are members of the Hague Apostille Convention. It verifies the authenticity of the signature, seal, or stamp on a public document — not the content of the document itself — so that it will be legally recognized in foreign countries.

Any country that has joined the Hague Apostille Convention accepts apostilles. There are more than 120 member countries, including most of Europe, Latin America, and many countries in Asia and Africa. If your destination country is not a member, a different process called embassy or consulate legalization is required. We can confirm which process applies to your situation.

A Hague Convention country is a nation that has signed and ratified the Hague Apostille Convention of 1961. This treaty creates a standardized process for authenticating public documents across member nations, replacing the need for multi-step embassy legalization between member countries. Common examples include Italy, Spain, Germany, France, Mexico, Brazil, and over 100 others.

It depends on where your document is going. If the destination country is a Hague Convention member, you need an apostille. If it is not a member — such as China (for certain purposes), Canada, or some Middle Eastern countries — you need embassy or consulate legalization, which is a separate multi-step process. We confirm which process applies to your specific situation as part of our review before any processing begins.
